01 · Overview

What it is.

Halldorophones are purpose‑built electro‑acoustic string instruments, based on the premise of using electronically induced feedback in a coupled system of strings — allowing for the creation of somewhat controlled, timbrally rich drones.

In the halldorophone a positive feedback loop is induced in a coupled system of eight strings via electromagnetic pickups and a speaker cone in the soundbox of the instrument. The vibration of each string is individually detected with a dedicated single‑coil pickup, the levels of which can be trimmed before being amplified and sent to the speaker. The speaker, in turn, vibrates the whole system — inducing feedback.

Mimicking the major characteristics of a cello — upright, four strings, fretless fingerboard, bowable string configuration — the halldorophone also has four sympathetic strings running below the fingerboard (much like a viola d'amore or a barytone) which are not directly accessible for bowing or plucking but are rather electronically excited to drone, most often via volume pedals.

By setting the volume level for the speaker, the player determines how aggressively the whole system is vibrating — riding the wave of feedback. All strings have the potential to be electronically included in or excluded from the feedback loop. The signal path can also be externally routed for DSP or pedal effects.

A note on practice
Feedback as method, not effect. String players have used feedback since electric guitars existed; the halldorophone is, perhaps, special because it is fundamentally configured around working with feedback — nudging the technique past "color" or "effect" toward becoming a method of approaching string playing.

The halldorophone (/ˈhældɔːrɔːfoʊn/; also known as the dorophone, and dórófónn in Icelandic) is a cello‑like electronic instrument created by artist and designer Halldór Úlfarsson. The halldorophone is designed specifically to feedback the strings, making use of the phenomenon of positive feedback to incite the strings to drone.

The instrument gained recognition in early 2020 when composer Hildur Guðnadóttir won the Academy Award for Best Original Score for her soundtrack to the film Joker, parts of which were composed with a halldorophone.

Description

The halldorophone is an electroacoustic instrument that makes use of positive feedback as a key element in generating its sound. The player takes a sitting position behind the instrument and plays it in a similar way as they would a cello. The instrument is predicated on audio feedback that is generated when sound picked up by a microphone (or comparable device) is passed to a speaker and then re‑detected by the microphone.

While every musical performance is unique in some way and contingent on its particular moment in time, the unstable nature of the halldorophone exacerbates this condition.

History

The halldorophone was first created around 2005 as an exploration of feedback and how it acts on the resonant body of a string instrument. Since then its creator has iterated through various designs, exploring the ergonomics and control features most appropriate for such an instrument.

Iterations

As the design has evolved the instrument has taken on a cello‑like form, with sympathetic strings — either two or four — running under the fingerboard. In a later revision, an embedded Bela single‑board computer running Pure Data was added, opening the instrument to on‑board digital signal processing.

Notable works and players

Several composers of the Icelandic S.L.Á.T.U.R. collective have used halldorophones in their works after the Hljóðheimar exhibition in Reykjavík, 2011. Hildur Guðnadóttir's collaboration with the late Jóhann Jóhannsson on the score for Arrival features the instrument prominently, as does the experimental metal group Sunn O))) on the track Novae.
